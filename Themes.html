Thematic Analysis

Corruption of Power

▼

One of the central themes in Animal Farm is how power corrupts and absolute power corrupts absolutely. The pigs, especially Napoleon, gradually assume more power and privilege, eventually betraying the very principles of the revolution they led.

Orwell demonstrates how the ideals of equality and shared prosperity are subverted when leaders prioritize their own interests over the common good. The progression from "All animals are equal" to "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others" encapsulates this corruption.

The Commandments

The Seven Commandments are gradually altered to suit the pigs' increasing privileges, showing how principles can be manipulated to maintain power.

Special Privileges

The pigs move into the farmhouse, sleep in beds, drink alcohol, and wear clothes—all activities initially forbidden as "human" behaviors.

Propaganda and Manipulation

▼

Orwell explores how language can be weaponized to control populations. Squealer, Napoleon's propagandist, uses rhetorical techniques to confuse, intimidate, and manipulate the other animals, demonstrating how those in power can use language to maintain control.

The constant revision of history, particularly regarding Snowball's role in the revolution and the Battle of the Cowshed, shows how controlling the narrative of the past helps secure power in the present.

Squealer's Tactics

Squealer uses complicated language, appeals to fear, and rhetorical questions to confuse and persuade the animals to accept the pigs' decisions.

Rewriting History

Snowball's contributions are erased and he is transformed from hero to villain to consolidate Napoleon's power and explain away failures.

Revolution and Idealism

▼

Animal Farm examines how revolutionary ideals can be corrupted. The initial revolution is driven by Old Major's vision of equality and freedom, but these noble goals are gradually abandoned as the new leaders become corrupted by power.

The novel suggests that revolutions often fail not because their ideals are flawed, but because those who gain power in the aftermath betray those ideals for personal gain.

Old Major's Speech

The idealistic vision that inspires the revolution emphasizes equality, shared labor, and freedom from human exploitation.

Betrayal of Boxer

Boxer, who embodies the loyal revolutionary spirit with his motto "I will work harder," is ultimately betrayed and sent to the knacker when he can no longer work.

Class and Inequality

▼

Despite the revolution's promise to eliminate class distinctions, Animal Farm quickly develops a new class system with the pigs as the privileged elite. This reflects Orwell's critique of how revolutionary movements often reproduce the very inequalities they sought to abolish.

The stratification of animal society—with pigs at the top, followed by dogs as enforcers, and the other animals as workers—mirrors class divisions in human society.

Education Inequality

The pigs' literacy gives them power over the other animals, allowing them to control information and manipulate the commandments.

Resource Distribution

Food rations decrease for working animals while increasing for the pigs, showing how resources are unequally distributed based on class.
